ARIMA Forecasting — UK Weekly Fruit & Vegetable Prices

Data and Stationarity

Following aggregation to a single mean price per week, the series comprises 527 observations spanning January 2015 to February 2026 — a clean weekly aggregate, resolving the 8,308-observation issue present in the Holt-Winters run. The ADF test on levels returned a Dickey-Fuller statistic of −5.92 (p < 0.01), rejecting the null of a unit root and indicating the series is stationary in levels. This is somewhat unusual for a price series but plausible here given the relatively short 11-year window and the mean-reverting nature of agricultural commodity prices around a long-run trend. Accordingly, no differencing was applied (d = 0), which auto.arima confirmed in its model search.

Model Selection

The auto.arima search evaluated 22 candidate specifications across ARIMA and seasonal ARIMA combinations, using AICc as the selection criterion. The selected model was ARIMA(2,0,0) with non-zero mean — a second-order autoregressive process with no moving average component and no differencing. The estimated coefficients are AR(1) = 0.677 (s.e. 0.042) and AR(2) = 0.230 (s.e. 0.042), both significant, with a long-run mean of 1.329. The positive and decaying AR coefficients indicate that this week’s price is strongly influenced by the previous two weeks, with the effect attenuating over time — a classic persistence structure for commodity prices. The model’s AIC of −18.75 was the lowest of all candidates evaluated.

The Auto ARIMA model achieves the best AIC by a substantial margin and the best MAE and MASE. The SARIMA model matches it almost exactly on RMSE (0.2355 vs 0.2356) but carries a much higher AIC due to the additional seasonal parameters — suggesting the seasonal structure it captures does not justify the added complexity. The STL + ARIMA is the weakest on all in-sample accuracy metrics, though this is expected since STL first removes the trend-seasonal component before ARIMA fits the residuals, which limits in-sample fit.

All three MASE values are well below 1.0, confirming that every model substantially outperforms a naïve random walk forecast — a meaningful benchmark for evaluation.

Residual Diagnostics

The Ljung-Box test results require careful interpretation because two different tests were applied. The checkresiduals() function uses a lag of approximately 2× the model order plus the seasonal period (104 lags total), which is a stringent test. Under this specification, the Auto ARIMA residuals show significant autocorrelation (Q* = 139.37, df = 102, p = 0.008) and STL + ARIMA residuals show highly significant autocorrelation (Q* = 248.44, df = 98, p ≈ 0). This indicates residual structure remains at longer lags — almost certainly the seasonal pattern at lag 52, which neither model fully captures.

However, when the Ljung-Box test is applied at the more conventional lag of 20, both models pass cleanly: Auto ARIMA (p = 0.619) and STL + ARIMA (p = 0.442) both indicate white noise residuals at short to medium lags. This distinction matters for interpretation — the models capture the short-run autocorrelation structure well, but leave some long-run seasonal signal in the residuals. For a 52-week forecast horizon this is a known limitation of ARIMA applied to weekly data, and is the reason STL decomposition is generally recommended for weekly series despite its weaker in-sample fit.

Forecast

The ARIMA(2,0,0) 52-week ahead forecast projects a gradual mean reversion toward the long-run level of approximately 1.33, with prediction intervals widening smoothly over the horizon. Because d = 0 and the AR coefficients sum to less than 1 (0.677 + 0.230 = 0.907), the forecast is stationary and will always revert to the mean rather than trending indefinitely — appropriate behaviour for an agricultural price index. The 95% prediction interval spans roughly ±0.5 price index points at the 52-week horizon.

The STL + ARIMA forecast captures the seasonal pattern explicitly in the forecast trajectory, producing a more textured forward path that rises and falls with the estimated 52-week seasonal cycle. This is arguably more realistic for a food price series with genuine seasonal agricultural patterns, even though its in-sample fit is weaker.

Model Recommendation

For point forecast accuracy and parsimony, ARIMA(2,0,0) is the best performing model by AIC, MAE, and MASE, and its residuals are well-behaved at the standard lag-20 threshold. However, for a report communicating forecast uncertainty over a full year ahead, the STL + ARIMA forecast is more interpretable and economically defensible — it produces a seasonally-shaped forecast path rather than a flat mean reversion, and the STL decomposition framework explicitly handles the weekly frequency that causes ETS and standard ARIMA to ignore seasonality entirely. The two models are best presented together: ARIMA(2,0,0) as the statistically optimal specification, and STL + ARIMA as the seasonally-aware forecast for stakeholder communication.
